When I'm preparing for a concert, the days before are all about immersion. I listen to the piece on repeat, read about its background, and  let myself get pulled into its world. Then there’s the input from the choir director -always insightful - which helps me connect to the music on a deeper level and perform with more intention.

A few days before a concert I like to give a “listening guide” to some lucky person, usually a close friend: it’s my way of sharing the energy, the emotion, the sheer beauty of the music. I can’t keep it all to myself.

Sometimes, like with the masterpiece I’m writing about today, I go further, writing the whole thing down for the people coming to the concert - what a nice cadeau. It's yours now.

# Historical context
Bach’s _St. John Passion_ (BWV 245) isn’t just brilliant - it’s bold, raw, and deeply tied to the ritual life of 18th-century Leipzig. Premiered on Good Friday, 1724, it was part of a long-standing tradition of Passion music performed during Vespers. This wasn’t a one-off: during his years in Leipzig, Bach programmed twenty-six Passion performances, including St. Matthew, St. Markus and the misattributed St. Lukas. _St. John_ was performed at least four times: quite understandable, when something hits that hard, you bring it back.
# The Setting: sacred, structured, intense
Leipzig didn’t mess around when it came to Holy Week. Palm Sunday meant _St. Matthew_. Good Friday? _St. John_. Bach, as Thomaskantor, was composing but also choosing the right piece for the moment, whether it was his own work or someone else’s. The _St. John Passion_ fit the Good Friday vibe perfectly: dramatic, fast-moving, and emotionally charged. It was the soundtrack to collective reflection, grief, and awe.

>🐣**Fun fact: writing for young voices, masterfully**
>
>Bach didn’t have a professional adult choir at his disposal - he had the [Thomanerchor](https://bach.org/bravo-thomanerchor/): boys between the ages of 9 and 18, with all the vocal range, energy, and unpredictability that comes with that. But instead of limiting him, this shaped his compositional choices in fascinating ways. He wrote music that worked with their strengths (clear melodic lines, balanced voicing across soprano, alto, tenor, and bass) and avoided extremes that could strain developing voices. Yet he never compromised on complexity or expression. The counterpoint is still rich. The phrasing is still deeply expressive. These pieces were crafted with precision to challenge, develop, and elevate young singers. Bach was writing music that taught, inspired, and demanded full engagement. That's part of the genius.
# Inside the structure
Johannes-Passion sets the Passion story from John 18–19 to music with striking emotional intensity and dramatic pacing. Designed for Lutheran Vespers, the work is split into two parts - before and after the sermon - mirroring the structure of the service itself.

> 🐣**Fun fact: reviving forgotten sounds**
> 
> Bach revived old instruments like the viola da gamba and lute, prized for their rich tones. 

It’s a tapestry of forms, each playing a distinct role:

- **Biblical Narrative** – The Evangelist (tenor) carries the story, quoting directly from the Gospel of John, with occasional lines drawn from Matthew and Mark.
- **Chorales** – Lutheran hymns are woven throughout, grounding the drama in the congregation’s voice and tradition.
- **Arias and Ariosos** – These lyrical interludes pause the action, offering poetic reflections on the events, often paired with expressive instrumental solos.
- **Turba Choruses** – The choir shifts into character, becoming the shouting crowd, the soldiers, the high priests - turning collective fury into powerful and deeply human musical moments.

The Passion unfolds in five dramatic scenes, pulling us straight into the final, brutal hours of Christ’s life:

1. The quiet tension of Gethsemane shattered by the arrest.
2. Peter’s shame burning through his triple denial.
3. The uneasy theatre of Pilate’s court: questions, silence, a crowd growing louder.
4. The long climb to Golgotha and the unbearable stillness of the crucifixion.
5. A burial, quiet and heavy, closing the arc.

But what sets this Passion apart isn’t just its structure or pacing: it’s the emotional gravity. Bach gives us a fully human Jesus who bleeds, struggles, is conflicted. There’s no distant divinity here, but raw humanity: fear, love, betrayal, resignation. he story hits hard because it feels real. The emotions are timeless, and they still speak.

And then peace. The final chorale, **“Ach Herr, lass dein lieb Engelein,”** doesn’t conclude but release. A prayer, soft and soaring, that asks for angels to carry the soul home. Not a triumph, but quiet, intimate, deeply moving.

This was the final note of the 1724 version, the one many now consider definitive. But in 1725, Bach revised the ending, adding a fuller chorus - chorale finale. 
# Not just a narrator: Evangelist as witness
The Evangelist doesn't just recount, but becomes the pulse of the story. Set to recitativo secco, with only a basso continuo beneath him, the tenor Evangelist channels the Gospel of John with gripping immediacy. Luther’s text, already potent, is transformed into something visceral: a vocal tightrope walk between clarity and emotion. Each line he sings balances precision with pathos; he doesn’t describe events, he _inhabits_ them. His voice trembles with the urgency of the moment, giving the sacred narrative the immediacy of breaking news. Every pause aches. The result? A tension that never lets go.
# Choir as crowd and conscience
The powerful choir is the narrative’s echo, conscience, and eruption. One moment, it’s a roaring crowd, spitting judgment with terrifying unity; the next, it softens into chorales so intimate they feel like whispered prayers. This dual role is the heartbeat of the _Passion_: raw action and quiet reflection, fury and faith.
 
The _turba_ choruses inject a theatrical chaos that makes the biblical drama feel dangerously real. Then, just as swiftly, the same voices soften into chorales anchored in Lutheran tradition, offering spaces to breathe, reflect, grieve. These meditative interludes bind action to faith, and individual to collective experience. Framed by the opening cry of _“Herr, unser Herrscher”_ and the final stillness of _“Ruht wohl”_, the choir delivers a spiritual architecture. It plays every role, wears every face, and breaks the fourth wall - inviting the audience not just to listen, but to feel complicit, seen, moved.
